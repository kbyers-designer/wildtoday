1
Beloved Turtle Sanctuary Ecologist Killed in Israeli Strike

Mona Khalil, a 76-year-old Lebanese marine conservationist known for protecting turtle nesting sites near Tyre, has died from injuries sustained in an Israeli airstrike on her home, the Orange House Project. For decades, Khalil ran a sanctuary that welcomed volunteers and tourists to help monitor loggerhead and green sea turtles along Lebanon's Mediterranean coast. Her work earned her respect as one of Lebanon's most respected voices for marine conservation, despite repeated threats from conflict.

2
Scientists Preserve Vaquita Skeleton Using 3D Imaging

With only 7–10 vaquitas remaining in the wild—making them Earth's rarest marine mammal—researchers at Florida Atlantic University and collaborating institutions have digitally reconstructed a complete skeleton using advanced CT scanning and microscopy. The freely accessible 3D models will help scientists and museums study the critically endangered porpoise without risking damage to the few physical specimens that exist, and may support future conservation and breeding efforts.

3
Mass Mortality of Australian Seal Pups Linked to Bird Flu Outbreak

More than 13,000 southern elephant seal pups have died on Heard Island, an Australian territory 4,000 km southwest of Perth, in what scientists confirmed as a H5N1 bird flu outbreak. Death rates reached up to 97% in some locations—compared to a typical 5% in normal years. The virus has spread to six species on the island, including king penguins and Antarctic fur seals, likely arriving from the French subantarctic islands.

4
Scientists Map Three Times More Climate-Resilient Coral Reefs

Wildlife Conservation Society and Macquarie University researchers analyzing over 45,000 coral surveys identified 165,922 square kilometers of potentially climate-resilient reefs across 71 countries—three times more than previously estimated. Critically, fewer than 28% of these reefs fall within protected areas, leaving more than 119,000 square kilometers vulnerable to fishing, pollution, and development, and representing a key opportunity for urgent conservation action.

5
Two Northwest Wildflowers Petitioned for Endangered Species Protection

The Center for Biological Diversity has petitioned the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service to list Palouse goldenweed and rough goldenweed—rare wildflowers native to the Pacific Northwest—under the Endangered Species Act. Only 0.1% of their original habitat remains due to agricultural conversion. Invasive species and slow federal listing timelines pose ongoing threats, though the petition will boost public awareness among landowners and agencies.

6
Rice, Corn, and Cassava Drive 11% of Global Deforestation

A study published in Nature Food reveals that three staple crops—rice, corn, and cassava—were responsible for approximately 11% of global deforestation between 2001 and 2022, accounting for 121 million hectares of forest loss and 41.2 gigatonnes of carbon emissions. Unlike concentrated crops like palm oil or soy, these staple foods' impact spans the globe, making them largely absent from climate and deforestation policy discussions that typically focus on beef and commodity exports.

7
Honeybees Blend Nutritious Baby Food to Optimize Larval Diets

University of Oxford researchers have discovered that honeybees blend pollen from multiple flowers into 'bee bread' and process it into royal jelly—a specialized secretion that provides larvae with balanced essential amino acids. Adult bees regulate their own intake to compensate for nutritional gaps in individual pollen sources. The findings underscore the importance of diverse, nutrient-rich pollen sources for wild bees and highlight the need for thoughtful pollinator habitat design.

8
Nigerian Biologist Rediscovers Critically Endangered Bat Species

Iroro Tanshi, a Nigerian conservation biologist, rediscovered the short-tailed roundleaf bat—feared extinct until her 2016 sighting in Nigeria's Afi Mountain Wildlife Sanctuary. Weighing as much as a teaspoon of salt, the species had not been recorded since the 1970s. Tanshi founded the Small Mammal Conservation Organisation (Smacon) and launched a successful wildfire prevention campaign, reducing forest blazes by over 80% in five years while establishing the only known protected colony of the species.

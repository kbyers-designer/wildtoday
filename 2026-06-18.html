Today's Briefing
1
Arizona invests $700,000 annually in wildlife crossings to reduce collisions

Arizona Governor Katie Hobbs and the state legislature have approved dedicated recurring funding for planning and constructing wildlife crossing infrastructure. The initiative tackles an urgent safety problem: approximately 1,270 reported wildlife-vehicle collisions occur annually in Arizona, with rural counties like Coconino accounting for 30% of crashes. These crossings are proven to reduce dangerous collisions while reconnecting animal habitat across highways cutting through key migration routes.

2
Colombia passes landmark law requiring cattle traceability to curb Amazon deforestation

Colombia enacted a historic law requiring cattle to be tracked from farm to supermarket, addressing deforestation tied to livestock production—the leading driver of rainforest tree loss. The measure came after investigations revealed Colombian grocery stores were unknowingly selling beef from cattle raised on illegally deforested land in national parks. The law will roll out over two years and mirror European Union deforestation-free commodity requirements, potentially serving as a model for the broader Amazon region.

3
Ancient Amazon farming system demonstrates sustainable food production

Chagra farming—a 4,500-year-old indigenous practice in the Colombian Amazon—produces food without pesticides while preserving forest. These small, biodiverse plots incorporate up to 104 crop species alongside native trees and are returned to the forest after five or six years. Research shows chagras store more carbon than monoculture plantations and support twice the biodiversity of conventional agriculture, yet they now face threats from mining, deforestation, and climate change.

4
Feral horse population explodes in Australian national park amid ecosystem collapse

The Invasive Species Council has warned that feral horse numbers in Kosciuszko National Park have surged 30% in just two years to approximately 18,000 animals. Invasive species are driving over 80% of Australia's most vulnerable native plants and animals toward extinction, with feral animals, insects, and weeds destroying habitat and native ecosystems across the continent.

5
Feral deer could cost Tasmania $1.4 billion over three decades, new analysis shows

Major economic modeling released by the Invasive Species Council warns that feral deer could impose up to $1.4 billion in costs to Tasmania over the next 30 years unless governments dramatically scale up control efforts. The modeling underscores the mounting economic and ecological toll of invasive species threatening Australia's natural heritage and agricultural systems.

6
Kangaroo Island declared feral pig-free, marking major conservation victory

After years of intensive management, Kangaroo Island has been declared feral pig-free, representing one of Australia's most significant invasive species success stories. The achievement demonstrates what can be accomplished when governments back science-based action and commit sustained resources to invasive species eradication, providing hope for similar efforts across the continent.

7
Three bats test positive for rabies at South Dakota national park

Wind Cave National Park in South Dakota has identified three bats that tested positive for rabies since May, with park staff finding deceased bats near the Elevator Building and Visitor Center, and a third at the campground. Although fewer than 1% of wild bats typically carry rabies, the disease is fatal without prompt medical treatment; the park is coordinating with the South Dakota Department of Health to monitor the situation.

8
Indigenous territorial recognition in Colombia and Ecuador strengthens traditional conservation

Indigenous Territorial Entities (ITEs) in Colombia and the newly recognized "chakra-produced certification" in Ecuador are protecting traditional farming systems that have proven vital for preventing deforestation and preserving biodiversity. Research shows territories managed by indigenous peoples are among the most effective ways of preventing tropical forest loss, with Kallari cooperative in Ecuador generating $2 million annually while maintaining 104 crop species and 80–150 companion plants per plot.

Today's Briefing
1
H5N1 Bird Flu Detected in Mainland Australia for First Time

Australia's agriculture authorities are investigating mainland Australia's first suspected case of the deadly H5N1 virus after a seabird tested positive in Western Australia. A second bird is also being tested, though officials report no evidence of infection in poultry or widespread mortality. The discovery raises concerns about disease spread as bird flu continues circulating globally.

2
Brazil Carves Amazon National Park to Make Room for Grain Railway

Brazil is reducing the boundaries of an Amazon national park to allow construction of a new railway to transport grain exports from the country's interior. The decision undermines forest protection in a region already facing pressure from soybean cultivation and infrastructure expansion, threatening protected areas that are vital to both biodiversity and climate stability.

3
Utah Secures 50,000-Acre Wildlife Management Area in Book Cliffs

The Utah Division of Wildlife Resources finalized the purchase of a 50,000-acre parcel in the Book Cliffs roadless area, which will become part of a wildlife management area for hunting, fishing and public recreation. The $50 million land acquisition from the Utah School and Institutional Trust Lands Administration ensures habitat for deer, elk, bison, black bear and Colorado River cutthroat trout, demonstrating a win for both conservation and education funding.

4
South African Men Sentenced in World's Largest Rhino Horn Trafficking Case

Multiple men in South Africa have been sentenced in what authorities are calling the 'world's largest' rhino horn trafficking case, highlighting the ongoing crisis of poaching and illegal wildlife trade. Rhino populations remain critically endangered, with fewer than 27,000 individuals remaining globally—less than 6% of historical numbers.

5
Mountain Gorillas Face New Threat as Ebola Cases Climb in DRC

Critically endangered mountain gorillas in the Democratic Republic of Congo and Uganda face a catastrophic risk as Ebola cases rise in the region. Scientists warn that if even one gorilla contracted the virus, the remaining populations of just over 1,000 individuals could suffer devastating losses, with projections suggesting fewer than 20% would survive infection.

6
East African Oil Pipeline Threatens Wetlands and Wildlife Corridors

The proposed East African Crude Oil Pipeline poses a severe threat to wetlands, wildlife corridors and ecosystems across multiple countries in the region, according to a new report. The infrastructure project could fragment critical habitats and disrupt migration routes for countless species in one of the world's most biodiverse regions.

7
Kenya Advances Fisheries Sustainability Through Enhanced Data Collection

Facing declining fish stocks, Kenya is boosting data collection efforts on its artisanal fishing fleet to improve management and sustainability of the nation's floundering fisheries. Better information on catches and fishing practices could help the country balance food security with long-term conservation of marine resources that support millions of people.

8
Pink Cockatoos Losing Habitat to Successive Bushfires in Victoria

Two major bushfires in twelve years have devastated critical habitat for endangered pink cockatoos in Victoria's Wyperfeld National Park, with nearly all mature native pine trees wiped out in the key breeding ground. The losses underscore how climate-driven wildfires are eroding habitat for vulnerable species already on the brink of extinction.

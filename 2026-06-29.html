1
Honduras Study Shows Camera Traps Reveal True Culprit Behind Crop Damage

In eastern Honduras, researchers from the Wildlife Conservation Society deployed camera traps to identify which animals were actually damaging cassava crops—and found surprising results. Contrary to local belief that endangered Baird's tapirs were the main culprits, the cameras revealed that Honduran cottontail rabbits were the most frequently recorded crop raiders. The findings, published in Neotropical Biology and Conservation, highlight how non-invasive monitoring can prevent unnecessary persecution of threatened species and point toward targeted, evidence-based conservation solutions.

2
Oregon Winery Brings Endangered Butterfly Back From the Brink

Van Duzer Vineyards in Oregon's Willamette Valley partnered with the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service to restore habitat for the Fender's blue butterfly, an endangered species once believed extinct. The innovative approach uses sheep grazing to manage dense vegetation, replacing fossil-fuel-powered equipment while creating conditions for native flowering plants. After years without sightings, scouts discovered an adult female butterfly on the property in 2014—a breakthrough that demonstrates how working lands and conservation can coexist.

3
Palmyra Atoll Becomes Global Model for Climate-Resilient Conservation

The Nature Conservancy and partners continue developing groundbreaking conservation research at Palmyra Atoll, a remote Pacific sanctuary located 1,000 miles south of Hawaiʻi. Scientists have created digital reef models to predict climate impacts and identify heat-resistant corals, technologies now protecting reefs across the Pacific including Maui and Indonesia. The atoll's research also addresses marine threats from fishing aggregating devices (FADs) and uses machine learning to monitor coral health—proving that small, protected areas can yield solutions with global reach.

4
H5N1 Bird Flu Reaches Australia, Raising Concern for Wildlife and Pets

The highly pathogenic H5N1 avian influenza has arrived on Australia's mainland, with confirmed cases detected in Western Australia and South Australia, primarily in seabirds. Authorities warn the virus poses serious risks to native wildlife and pet cats and dogs that may contact infected birds. The Australian government identified over 150 native bird species and more than 10 mammal species—including sea lions and fur seals—at very high risk of extinction or major decline should the virus spread widely.

5
Offshore Oil Expansion Threatens Whale Corridors and Coral Reefs

A major analysis by Earth Insight reveals that over a quarter of marine protected areas across 11 countries overlap with zones designated for oil and gas development, as do 40% of coral reefs and nearly a third of mangrove forests. In Kenya, proposed offshore blocks cover 100% of coastal coral reefs and productive fishing grounds. The Center for International Environmental Law warns the offshore oil industry is pursuing the ocean as the "next frontier," driven by resistance to terrestrial drilling and advances in deep-water technology.

6
Catalina Island Fox Population Rebounds to Historic Levels

The Catalina Island fox population has grown to an estimated 2,162 animals, up from just 1,974 the previous year—a remarkable recovery from a low of roughly 15 individuals just over 25 years ago. Once on the verge of extinction, the small endemic fox species is now thriving again thanks to decades of dedicated restoration work. Wildlife officials note, however, that while the population is recovering, a growing number of foxes are being killed by golden eagles, presenting new conservation challenges.

7
High Seas Treaty Commits Global Community to Protecting 30% of Ocean by 2030

The High Seas Treaty, a legally binding international agreement, establishes a global framework to place 30% of the world's oceans into protected areas by 2030. This commitment to "30×30" aligns with the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework and represents unprecedented cooperation to safeguard marine biodiversity and combat the growing threat of offshore fossil fuel extraction. The agreement signals a turning point in global ocean governance, providing countries with a roadmap to balance conservation and sustainable resource use.

8
Seychelles Designates 30% of Marine Zone as Protected Area

The island nation of Seychelles has achieved a major milestone by designating 30% of its exclusive economic zone as marine protected area, tripling the UN Convention on Biological Diversity target of 10% protection. The decision protects critical fish nurseries and supports coastal communities that depend on ocean resources for food and livelihoods. Seychelles' proactive approach demonstrates how small nations can lead large-scale ocean conservation and inspire regional cooperation in the Indian Ocean.

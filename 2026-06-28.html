Today's Briefing
1
North Atlantic Right Whales Show Strongest Breeding Success Since 2009

The 2026 calving season delivered exceptional news for North Atlantic right whales, with 23 calves born along the southeast coast—the highest number since 2009. Of these, 20 were returning mothers, demonstrating the species' gradual recovery from intensive conservation efforts. This milestone provides hope for a species that once numbered only a few hundred individuals.

2
Lawsuit Challenges Federal Delay on Sunflower Sea Star Protections

The Center for Biological Diversity sued the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration for missing a legal deadline to list sunflower sea stars as endangered. The keystone Pacific species lost 90% of its population to a bacterial wasting disease since 2013, with the southern range considered functionally extinct in California. Scientists are advancing captive breeding programs to help restore wild populations.

3
Namibia Commits to Conserving One-Quarter of Its Territory

Namibia has emerged as a global model for community-led conservation, pledging to protect 25% of its sparsely populated, wildlife-rich lands. The initiative puts conservation management directly in the hands of rural communities, creating sustainable livelihoods while preserving ecosystems and wildlife corridors essential to species survival.

4
French Polynesia Establishes Vast Marine Protected Area the Size of France

French Polynesia's government announced the creation of a new marine reserve encompassing 200,000 square miles around the Austral and Marquesas Islands—one of the single largest ocean protection commitments ever made. The biologically rich archipelagos will receive the highest level of marine protection, safeguarding critical habitat for thousands of species.

5
Urban Backyards Become Vital Bird Habitat as Cities Expand

Research from the University of Illinois-Chicago reveals that private backyards are major sources of bird habitat in urban environments, potentially offsetting habitat loss from sprawl. North America has lost 29% of its bird population over 50 years, but neighborhoods with diverse native vegetation, water sources, and brush piles show significantly higher bird diversity and can help reverse population declines.

6
California Condor Returns to Oregon After 122 Years

A California condor recently became the first free-flying member of the species documented in Oregon since 1904—a remarkable milestone for one of the world's rarest birds. Once numbering fewer than 30 individuals, the species has recovered through intensive captive breeding and reintroduction efforts, showing that recovery is possible with sustained commitment.

7
Record 602 Dams Removed Across Europe in 2025

Europe removed a record 602 dams in 2025—11% more than the previous year—as part of a global trend to restore rivers and their ecosystems. The removals allow migratory fish to reach critical spawning grounds and restore habitat connectivity, with positive effects rippling through entire river systems and surrounding wildlife communities.

8
River Wye Granted Legal Rights as Living Ecosystem

Britain's River Wye has been granted unprecedented legal rights in a landmark conservation decision, recognizing the entire 130-mile catchment as a living ecosystem with the right to flow, regenerate, support biodiversity, and be represented in decision-making. The action reflects a growing legal movement recognizing nature's intrinsic value beyond human use.

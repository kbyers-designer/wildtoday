H5 Bird Flu Detected on Australia's East Coast as Deadly Virus Spreads

New South Wales has recorded its first suspected case of deadly H5 bird flu in a giant petrel discovered near Hawks Nest north of Newcastle. If confirmed as the highly pathogenic H5N1 strain, it will mark the first detection on Australia's east coast. Five cases have already been detected in Western Australia and South Australia since late June in migratory sub-Antarctic birds. Authorities have been preparing surveillance systems for years and have activated a state coordination center to monitor for further spread, with testing by the Australian Centre for Disease Preparedness underway to confirm the strain.

2
Study Warns Screwworm Control Must Include Wildlife Surveillance

A new study published in Conservation Biology by the Wildlife Conservation Society warns that efforts to control New World screwworm will fail if governments focus only on livestock while ignoring illegal cattle movements, wildlife infections, and protected areas. Researchers found that many corridors used for illegal cattle trade pass through conservation lands, creating pathways for the parasitic fly to reach poorly monitored wildlife populations. The One Health approach linking animal, ecosystem, and human health is essential to prevent screwworm from devastating species of conservation concern across Mesoamerica and beyond.

3
Proposed Farm Bill Cuts Threaten Conservation Program Funding

Congressional proposals for the next Farm Bill would slash funding for crucial conservation programs like the Environmental Quality Incentives Program (EQIP) and Conservation Stewardship Program (CSP), according to the Institute for Agriculture and Trade Policy. Only 24% of farmers who applied for EQIP in fiscal year 2025 received funding, leaving 76% without support. The funding cuts, combined with USDA staff reductions, will dramatically reduce conservation practices farmers can implement during an already challenging economic period.

4
Livestock Population Surges 50% Globally, Straining Wildlife and Ecosystems

A major report from the Stop Financing Factory Farming alliance shows the number of farmed mammals and poultry has increased by half since 2006—adding 33 billion animals—while cropland used for animal feed has expanded by a quarter. This boom is intensifying pressure on natural systems, with 90% of water withdrawn for irrigation now used to grow livestock feed. Emissions from livestock have grown by more than a fifth since 2001, and agricultural runoff is creating vast dead zones in marine ecosystems. Experts call for a shift away from meat-heavy diets and for development banks to stop financing factory farming.

5
Environmental DNA Reveals Human Impact on Freshwater Fish Biodiversity

A new study across 113 river systems on five continents using environmental DNA (eDNA) shows how human activity and climate change are reshaping fish communities. Researchers at the University of Western Australia, University of Zurich, and Yunnan University analyzed nearly 2,000 freshwater sites—ecosystems that cover less than 1% of Earth but support 10% of all species. The analysis found that while climate drives natural biodiversity patterns, human activity weakens these relationships and reduces functional and phylogenetic diversity, suggesting human pressures override natural ecological processes in river catchments.

6
Fifty Years of Silence: Britain's Dawn Chorus Has Collapsed

The Guardian recreated Britain's dawn chorus from 1976 and compared it with recordings from today, revealing a devastating decline in birdsong over five decades. The analysis reflects the loss of approximately 73 million wild birds from the UK landscape, driven by habitat loss, pesticide use, and intensive agriculture. The loss of the morning symphony—once a defining feature of rural and suburban soundscapes—serves as an acoustic measure of the broader biodiversity crisis facing the nation's bird populations.

7
Dolphins in Adriatic Learn to Follow Fishing Trawlers for Food

Bottlenose dolphins in the overfished Adriatic Sea are increasingly relying on fishing trawlers for food, with 76% of boats in some areas being followed by dolphins that have learned the behavior from their parents. This dependency reflects the collapse of natural fish stocks due to intensive commercial fishing. Scientists warn that while dolphins may appear to benefit short-term, this scavenging behavior undermines natural hunting skills and indicates a broader marine ecosystem in crisis.

8
UK Watchdog: Farming Agency Broke Law Allowing Bee-Killing Pesticide

A UK watchdog has ruled that Defra (Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs) breached environmental law by permitting farmers to use a neonicotinoid pesticide known to harm bees and other pollinators. The decision highlights ongoing conflicts between agricultural interests and environmental protection, particularly regarding bee population decline and its impacts on crop pollination and ecosystem health. The ruling underscores the need for stronger enforcement of wildlife protections in farming policy.

1
Amazon deforestation falls to 10-year low in first half of 2026

The Brazilian Amazon recorded its lowest deforestation rate in a decade during the first half of 2026, with 1,295 square kilometers of forest loss—a 38% decrease compared to the same period in 2025. Scientists credit political will to fight deforestation, demonstrating that forest loss is not inevitable but responds to government policy decisions. However, threats from illegal gold mining, forest fires, and forecasts of a severe El Niño event pose ongoing risks to this progress.

2
Amazon soy pact collapse threatens 1.4 million hectares of new deforestation

A landmark agreement that dramatically reduced deforestation from soy farming in the Brazilian Amazon has collapsed, and a new study warns it will unleash at least 1.4 million hectares of additional forest loss by 2036. The Amazon Soy Moratorium, which successfully prevented new soy expansion on deforested land since 2006, fell apart due to legal challenges and state-level laws favoring agricultural interests. The projected carbon emissions would equal Canada's annual greenhouse gas output.

3
Trump administration narrows Endangered Species Act protections

The Trump administration finalized a rule narrowing the definition of 'harm' under the Endangered Species Act, opening critical wildlife habitats to oil, gas drilling, mining, and logging. The change redefines harm to exclude habitat degradation, upending 50 years of interpretation and 74 species protections in Arizona alone. Conservation organizations have filed lawsuits, and 99% of public comments opposed the change, though implementation begins in September.

4
West African cocoa boom destroying Liberia's forests at alarming pace

Mongabay's investigation reveals a "cocoa rush" devastating Liberia's remaining rainforests, driven by climate-disrupted yields in neighboring Côte d'Ivoire and surging cacao prices. Over 150,000 migrant workers have crossed into southeastern Liberia to farm cacao, with vast swaths of primary forest cleared in just the past two years. The emerging trade threatens the region's biodiversity and may be shut out of the European Union market under new deforestation regulations.

5
Invasive giant prawn colonizes Brazil's protected coastal ecosystems

An invasive giant river prawn introduced for aquaculture has escaped into Brazil's coastal protected areas, including UNESCO World Heritage Sites, according to a new study spanning 2015–2025. The highly adaptable species preys on native crustaceans, competes for food and shelter, and carries pathogens that threaten aquaculture and wild populations. Small-scale fishers report dramatic declines in native shrimp catches, threatening their livelihoods and food security.

6
Eastern US wildlife restoration shows dramatic success across species

A new book profiling America's eastern wildlife chronicles inspiring reintroduction and restoration successes, including Kentucky's now-largest elk herd east of the Mississippi (10,000 animals), bison returns to Illinois prairies, and the resurgence of moose, sandhill cranes, and red wolves. These projects demonstrate how conservation, policy, and community imagination can reverse centuries of habitat loss and species extinction, though ongoing sprawl and habitat fragmentation threaten long-term recovery.

7
Arctic community trials sea ice thickening to protect hunting traditions

A climate tech startup is pumping seawater onto Arctic sea ice in Nunavut, Canada, allowing it to refreeze and thicken the ice sheet—a geoengineering approach being tested with Inuit community partners. Early results show the treated ice is 50 centimeters thicker than surrounding ice and more reflective, slowing summer melt. The project aims to protect traditional hunting grounds and cultural practices threatened by accelerating ice loss, though scientists debate environmental risks.

8
Former park officials urge protection of Chaco Culture sacred landscape

Nearly 6,000 former National Park Service employees and rangers oppose the Trump administration's proposal to revoke protections for lands surrounding Chaco Culture National Historical Park in New Mexico. The area is sacred to Southwestern Tribes and contains thousands of Ancestral Puebloan cultural features threatened by oil and gas development. Over 90% of surrounding federal lands have already been leased for drilling, and the proposed rollback would allow further industrial encroachment on this World Heritage Site.

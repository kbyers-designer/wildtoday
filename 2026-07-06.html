DRC Community Forest Concession Cuts Deforestation by 87% in One Year

A displaced community in the Democratic Republic of Congo, now legally managing 71,700 acres as the Bamasobha forest concession, reduced deforestation by 87% in a single year through local patrols and forest management plans. Working with support organizations, the community divided their forest into zones for production, conservation, protection, and regeneration, proving that local stewardship delivers faster results than top-down approaches alone. The model is already spreading, with over 227 community forest concessions now established across the DRC, covering more than 11 million acres.

2
Water Voles Return to North Devon After 25+ Years

The National Trust has successfully reintroduced approximately 110 water voles to the Combesgate catchment in Woolacombe, Devon, more than 25 years after the species was declared functionally extinct in the region. The reintroduction followed extensive habitat restoration work including the creation of 20 ponds, planting of 26,000 trees, and 15 hectares of wildflower seeding. Water voles, which are the UK's fastest-declining mammal, play a crucial ecological role by creating burrows used by other species and serving as prey for herons, foxes, and other wildlife.

3
Pennsylvania Creates Multi-Agency Wildlife Crossings Strategy

Pennsylvania's Shapiro administration has announced a new inter-agency working group to develop a comprehensive strategy for reducing wildlife-vehicle collisions while reconnecting fragmented habitats. The state secured an $840,000 federal grant to fund development of the strategic plan, which will identify priority areas for wildlife crossings and habitat restoration. Pennsylvania motorists face a 1-in-62 chance of a collision with wildlife each year, making it the fifth riskiest state in the nation for such incidents.

4
Mink Eradication Project Could Cut Kent Population by 90%

The Waterlife Recovery Trust, with a £20,000 grant from the BASC Wildlife Fund, is expanding mink trapping and monitoring across Kent to reduce invasive American mink populations by 90% within two years. American mink prey heavily on water voles, the UK's fastest-declining mammal, as well as ground-nesting birds such as snipe, lapwing, and waterfowl. A similar project in East Anglia achieved a 70% year-on-year reduction in mink numbers, leading to complete removal from Norfolk, Suffolk, and East Cambridgeshire.

5
Gorilla Genetic Legacy Preserved Through Cryopreservation

Nature's SAFE, in partnership with the Bristol Zoological Society, has cryopreserved sperm and tissue samples from Jock, a 42-year-old western lowland gorilla who died in February. Live sperm cells have been frozen for future breeding programs, while preserved testicular tissue could potentially be used to grow new sperm, and stored bodily tissues could enable creation of genetically identical animals. Western lowland gorillas are critically endangered, with populations declining more than 60% over the past two decades.

6
Congress Acts to Protect Ocean Observatories Initiative Data

Congress has taken action to protect the Ocean Observatories Initiative after concerns were raised about potential dismantling of critical ocean monitoring infrastructure. The initiative provides vital climate and ocean data relied upon by scientists, coastal communities, and fisheries. The decision highlights the growing recognition that ocean data collection is essential to understanding marine ecosystems and addressing climate change impacts.

7
Marine Protected Areas Expand with Reef Check Monitoring Data

Reef Check Hong Kong data is helping guide new marine conservation measures, including proposals for 156 acres of protected marine park and a reservation system for heavily visited sites. The 2025 surveys documented coral cover ranging from 15.6% to 84.7%, with 12 sites recording more than 50% coverage, as well as localized bleaching and abandoned fishing gear. Long-term volunteer monitoring programs like Reef Check provide essential data that scientists, managers, and policymakers use to make informed conservation decisions.

8
Golden Lion Tamarin Population Rebounds to Over 4,800

The golden lion tamarin population in Brazil has recovered to over 4,800 individuals thanks to decades of targeted conservation efforts, after the species faced severe habitat loss that left fewer than 200 remaining in the wild. The remarkable turnaround demonstrates the power of sustained, community-based habitat protection and breeding programs in reversing species decline.

Today's Briefing
1
Public lands conservation rule rescinded

The Interior Department has finalized cancellation of the 2024 Public Lands Rule, which had put conservation and ecological restoration on equal legal footing with drilling, mining, logging, and grazing across roughly 245 million acres of Bureau of Land Management land. The repeal removes a formal program that allowed conservation leasing and instructed BLM to treat habitat, clean water, and cultural resources as co‑equal “uses,” returning priority to extractive industries despite previous findings that conservation had historically been marginalized.

2
Rapid expansion of hunting on federal lands

The Trump administration has ordered managers of about 70–76 national recreation areas, wildlife refuges, and other federal sites to roll back hunting and trapping restrictions, including bans on artificial lights, rules on permanent tree stands, and limits aimed at public safety. Conservation groups and park advocates warn that the changes were pushed through quickly, with limited impact assessment, and could increase pressure on wildlife and create more conflicts between hunters and other visitors on multi‑use lands.

3
Endangered species protections stripped in the Gulf

The renamed Endangered Species Committee, now informally dubbed the “Extinction Committee” or “God Squad,” approved a plan that removes key protections for endangered marine species in the Gulf of Mexico, including the critically rare Rice’s whale, in order to facilitate oil and gas activity. Advocacy groups describe the decision as prioritizing an “energy emergency” over one of the world’s smallest whale populations, with roughly 51 individuals thought to remain, and are mobilizing protests timed to Endangered Species Day and follow‑up actions today.

4
Global biodiversity framework and 2026 “triple‑COP” year

Governments met in Rome for the sixth meeting of the Subsidiary Body on Implementation (SBI‑6) of the Convention on Biological Diversity, launching the first global review of how countries are implementing the Kunming–Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework’s 23 targets. 2026 is being framed by UN biodiversity institutions as a “triple‑COP” year in which climate, biodiversity, and desertification summits will collectively assess whether current policies can realistically halt and reverse biodiversity loss by 2030, with COP17 on biodiversity in Armenia as a key milestone.

5
Wildlife connectivity and high‑seas protection bills

In the U.S., a bipartisan “Wildlife Corridors and Habitat Connectivity Conservation Act of 2026” has been introduced to create a National Wildlife Corridor System on federal lands and waters, with mapping, funding, and a mechanism to withdraw designated corridors from new mining and mineral leasing. In Australia, a High Seas Biodiversity Treaty Bill has entered parliament to implement the UN high‑seas agreement domestically, setting up a regulatory regime for marine genetic resources, new high‑seas marine protected areas, and environmental impact assessments in areas beyond national jurisdiction.

6
Texas Approves Changes to Hunting and Migratory Bird regulations

The Texas Parks and Wildlife Commission approved changes to the 2026-2027 Statewide Hunting and Migratory Bird regulations. Changes include an earlier dove hunting season, standardized bag limits, and shooting hours. Additionally, chachalaca and quail hunting seasons were aligned, and wild turkey hunting regulations were adjusted due to population declines.

7
Controversial Dam Threatens Pangolins

A proposed dam in Kathmandu’s northeastern ridge aims to revive the Bagmati River and alleviate water shortages but could harm the critically endangered Chinese pangolin and other wildlife in Shivapuri Nagarjun National Park. Conservationists argue the dam’s environmental impact assessment (EIA) overlooks these threats and call for stronger scrutiny of EIAs, which they claim often prioritize socio-economic benefits over biodiversity. The Asian Development Bank, funding the project, states its investments undergo detailed studies to ensure sustainability.

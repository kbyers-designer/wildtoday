1
Amazon Deforestation Hits 8-Year Low as Brazil Reports Historic Drop

Brazil's government announced a sharp decline in Amazon deforestation, with May 2026 clearing 61.4% lower than May 2025—the lowest ever recorded for that month. Over the 10-month period from August 2025 to May 2026, deforestation fell 36%, and Environment Minister João Paulo Capobianco said the nation is on track to reach its lowest annual deforestation level. The decline reflects strengthened enforcement by Brazil's environment ministry and partnerships with conservation groups, though experts warn that vigilance remains critical during the dry season.

2
Extreme Heat Now Killing Wildlife Across Globe, New Early Warning System Developed

Eight endangered Asiatic lions died in India from heat stroke, and heat waves have devastated species globally—from howler monkeys in Mexico to flying foxes in Australia. A new forecasting system published in Nature Climate Change uses satellite data to predict when 30,000+ species will face extreme temperatures never previously experienced in their ranges. The system could have warned of 3,500+ species at risk 3–5 months in advance during 2024–2025, potentially saving lives through preventive wildlife interventions.

3
Human-Wildlife Conflict Escalates in Connecticut as Bear Population Thrives

Connecticut's Department of Energy and Environmental Protection reports responding to over 100 nuisance bear complaints so far in 2026, with 20 home break-ins already—matching the pace of record-breaking 2024. Jenny Dickson, director of wildlife for the state agency, warns that an increasing number of bears are becoming food-conditioned and habituated to people, a combination that creates dangerous scenarios for both humans and bears. Officials encourage residents to secure trash, use noise deterrents, and take swift action to discourage bears from neighborhoods.

4
Three Bats Test Positive for Rabies at Wind Cave National Park

The National Park Service announced three bats recently tested positive for rabies at Wind Cave National Park in South Dakota, with one found deceased near the Elevator Building and another discovered alive but unable to fly. Although less than 1% of bats in nature typically carry rabies, the disease is fatal without prompt medical treatment. The NPS is coordinating with South Dakota Department of Health to monitor public safety and has reminded visitors to avoid direct contact with bats, alive or dead.

5
Connecticut Audubon Migration Magic Raises $30,000 for Bird Conservation

Connecticut Audubon's Migration Magic celebration attracted more than 1,200 participants during its month-long event, raising nearly $30,000 to support bird conservation and habitat restoration. The campaign connected birders and nature lovers across the state to celebrate migratory bird populations and fund education and research efforts. The fundraiser underscores growing public enthusiasm for avian conservation during peak migration season.

6
Liberty Wildlife Handles Record Intake Surge Amid Arizona Summer Heat

Liberty Wildlife, Arizona's largest wildlife rehabilitation center, has received 6,745 animals year-to-date—far exceeding typical numbers as the region enters peak heat season. Recent cases include a three-legged kit fox recovering from a trap injury and numerous drought-stressed Gambel's quail with failed breeding seasons. With nearly 400 dedicated volunteers, the organization emphasizes the critical role of public reporting and community care in wildlife survival during extreme environmental conditions.

7
USDA's Natural Resources Conservation Service Updates Strategic Priorities

The U.S. Department of Agriculture's Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS) announced updated conservation priorities and a renewed "Farmers First" mission to advance stewardship of working lands. The agency is focusing on supporting private landowners and ranchers through programs like the Conservation Stewardship Program and the Sentinel Landscapes Partnership, which protects military mission-critical areas. NRCS also expanded assistance to producers facing challenges from invasive species and climate-related threats like drought and wildfires.

8
New Research Calls for Animal Movement Data in Global Biodiversity Tracking

A study published in Nature argues that animal movement patterns must be integrated into official biodiversity indicators to better track ecosystem health and conservation progress. Researchers emphasize that migration routes and range shifts—driven by climate change, habitat loss, and other pressures—are critical to understanding species survival but are often overlooked in policy frameworks. Incorporating animal movement data would provide governments and conservation groups with more dynamic, real-time insights for wildlife management.

Today's Briefing
1
Colossal Biosciences and U.S. Government Team Up on Endangered Species Gene Bank

The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service has announced a partnership with de-extinction biotech firm Colossal Biosciences to preserve genetic material from all 2,300+ species listed under the Endangered Species Act. The effort will create a comprehensive genome bank and tissue repository in Dallas, with duplicate samples distributed across the country, providing conservationists with biological "backups" for recovery and restoration efforts. All genomic data will be deposited into open-access repositories available to the global scientific community at no cost.

2
Ospreys in Chesapeake Bay Starving at Disastrous Rates

After a remarkable recovery from DDT-era declines, osprey populations in the Chesapeake Bay watershed have plummeted sharply in recent years, with birds starving at alarming rates. Audubon scientists report that the osprey's fate may once again rest on collective action and habitat restoration, as environmental stressors and declining fish availability threaten the species' continued recovery across the region.

3
International Marine Expedition Uncovers 31 New Species in Two Weeks Off Brazil

International marine researchers have discovered 31 otherworldly new species during a two-week expedition in the midwater region of the South Atlantic Ocean off Brazil, including transparent squid and bizarre crustaceans never before documented. The findings underscore the urgency of exploring and cataloging ocean biodiversity before species are lost to environmental change, with global biodiversity efforts racing to accelerate species discovery.

4
Bipartisan America the Beautiful Act Advances in House Committee

The U.S. House Committee on Natural Resources has advanced the bipartisan America the Beautiful Act of 2026, which reauthorizes the National Parks and Public Land Legacy Restoration Fund through fiscal year 2031. The measure directs nearly $2 billion to address deferred maintenance on federal lands managed by the National Park Service, U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, USDA Forest Service, and Bureau of Land Management, enabling critical habitat restoration and forest management projects.

5
Scientist Wins $100,000 Prize for Breakthrough in Decoding Birdsong

A researcher has won a prestigious $100,000 award for developing new methods to decode avian communication, achieving a significant step forward in understanding how birds communicate and interact with their environments. The breakthrough in human-animal communication could help conservationists better protect endangered bird species by revealing previously hidden details about their behavior, habitat needs, and responses to environmental change.

6
AI Projects Revolutionize Bird Conservation Efforts

Artificial intelligence is transforming bird conservation across five major research initiatives, with scientists using AI to analyze troves of images and audio recordings to gain unprecedented insights into avian ecology and population dynamics. These machine learning tools are helping conservationists identify endangered birds in the field, track migration patterns, and prioritize habitat protection efforts more efficiently than traditional methods.

7
H5N1 Bird Flu Threatens Tasmanian Devils, Rare Parrots in Australia

As H5N1 bird flu spreads across Australian wildlife, researchers warn that iconic species including Tasmanian devils, swift parrots, and black swans face unprecedented risk from the virus. With confirmed cases already reported in South Australia and Western Australia, wildlife officials are preparing emergency response protocols to prevent catastrophic population declines in species already threatened by habitat loss.

8
Colombian Rice Fields Become Refuge for Declining Lesser Yellowlegs

Biologists are conducting conservation fieldwork in Colombia's rice-growing regions to protect the endangered Lesser Yellowlegs, a small migratory shorebird whose population has declined sharply in recent decades. These croplands, which provide crucial stopover habitat during migration, are being studied for their potential role in supporting the species' survival, with wildlife researchers banding birds and gathering data to improve protection strategies.

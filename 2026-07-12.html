Today's Briefing
1
Trump Administration Narrows Endangered Species Act "Harm" Definition

The Trump administration finalized a rule on July 10 that redefines "harm" under the Endangered Species Act, the landmark 1973 conservation law. The new definition eliminates protections for habitat destruction alone, allowing drilling, mining, and logging on critical wildlife habitats so long as animals themselves aren't directly killed or injured. Environmental groups warn the move could cause species extinction by removing one of the most effective wildlife protection tools, while the administration claims it restores the law's original intent and protects private property rights.

2
US Official With Family Commercial Breeding Ties to Lead CITES Delegation

Jennifer Chatfield, a high-ranking Department of Interior official, will reportedly lead the U.S. delegation to the CITES Animals Committee meeting in Geneva next week. Chatfield's family owns a Florida breeding facility for critically endangered lemurs, and she faces ongoing ethics investigations for potential conflicts of interest. Conservation experts worry the political appointee may undermine the scientific integrity of the global wildlife trade meeting and favor industry interests over conservation.

3
Amazon Deforestation Hits Lowest Level in a Decade

Deforestation in Brazil's Amazon rainforest fell to its lowest level in more than a decade, with satellite data showing a 37% decrease in forest loss compared to the previous year. The National Space Research Institute attributed the decline to government actions protecting the forest and combating environmental crime. President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva has pledged to eradicate illegal deforestation by 2030, marking a reversal from policies under his predecessor Jair Bolsonaro.

4
UK Implements New Deforestation Ban in Supply Chains, Aligns with EU Standards

The UK government announced new rules requiring companies to exclude illegal deforestation from their supply chains, aligning closely with EU Deforestation Regulation standards. The policy applies similar commodities, reporting requirements, and a £1 million turnover threshold. Conservation groups noted that while the announcement is significant, implementation requires secondary legislation and proper resourcing to be effective in preventing supply chain deforestation linked to habitat destruction.

5
Lydia Möcklinghoff, Giant Anteater Champion, Dies in Plane Crash at 45

Lydia Möcklinghoff, a German biologist and science communicator, died on July 3 in a plane crash near Campo Grande, Brazil, while conducting fieldwork in the Pantanal wetland. Over two decades, Möcklinghoff became one of Europe's leading experts on giant anteaters, combining rigorous field research with accessible public communication through books, radio, podcasts, and children's science programs. Her work linked anteater behavior to habitat loss, fire, and drought, helping overlooked species gain recognition and protection.

6
Scientists Map Global Seagrass Ecosystems Using AI and Satellite Imagery

Arizona State University researchers used artificial intelligence and satellite data to create the first high-resolution global map of seagrass ecosystems. The study found that 70% of seagrass cover is concentrated off just five countries' coasts, yet only 21% falls within marine protected areas. While 4% of seagrass was lost over four years, improved water quality in some regions shows recovery is possible—offering hope for conservation efforts targeting these critical carbon-sequestering ecosystems.

7
Pangolin Trafficking Ring Busted in Jakarta; 3-Ton Scale Seizure Leads to Arrest

Indonesian customs officers charged a suspect and launched a manhunt following the seizure of nearly 3 tons of pangolin scales intended for smuggling to Cambodia. The bust represents one of the largest recent seizures of the world's most trafficked mammal. Pangolins, critically endangered due to illegal wildlife trade, are hunted for their scales (used in traditional medicine) and meat, making enforcement actions like this crucial to preventing species extinction.

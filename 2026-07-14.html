Today's Briefing
1
JBS Abandons Net-Zero and Deforestation Commitments

The world's largest meat company dropped its ambitious climate pledges in its latest sustainability report. JBS removed references to its 2040 net-zero emissions target and zero-deforestation goals after claiming for years that reducing greenhouse gas emissions was a key strategic objective. The company cited the challenge of tracking emissions across hundreds of thousands of independent agricultural producers as the reason for the retreat, shifting focus instead to only its direct operations—which represent just 2-3% of total emissions. Environmental groups argue the move reveals how major corporations use net-zero commitments strategically to access capital and favorable stock listings before abandoning them.

2
Trump Administration Removes 50-Year-Old Habitat Protections for 1,300+ Endangered Species

The Interior and Commerce departments finalized a rule rescinding the definition of "harm" under the Endangered Species Act, a foundational interpretation that has protected species habitats since 1975. The change means habitat destruction is no longer legally considered "harm" to endangered species—only direct killing or injury is prohibited. Conservation groups argue habitat loss is the number one cause of extinction and warn that logging, mining, and development can now proceed in critical ecosystems. Earthjustice and other organizations have already announced plans to challenge the rule in federal court.

3
Climate Change Is Making Invasive Pike Hungrier and More Dangerous in Alaska

Warming waters in Alaska's Southcentral region are causing invasive northern pike to become more aggressive predators, consuming 63% more fish than they did a decade ago. Research from the University of Alaska Fairbanks shows pike metabolisms are accelerating in warming lakes, with projections of 6-12% increases in appetite by 2100. The invasive predators have devastated native salmon runs, causing millions in lost sportfishing revenue and forcing dozens of remote fishing lodges to close. Recent studies also show pike can traverse saltwater, potentially allowing them to colonize new waterways across the state.

4
West Susitna Valley Faces Record Extinction Risk for Iconic Salmon Runs

The Deshka River and Susitna drainage have been closed to king salmon sport fishing for the first time in recent history due to record-low returns. State biologists attribute the collapse to multiple stressors: warming waters, pike predation, competition from hatchery salmon, ocean bycatch, and predation by orcas. The West Susitna Valley—once home to two dozen world-class fishing lodges and serving as economic anchor for remote communities—has transformed into what one Alaska Department of Fish & Game biologist candidly called a "growing dumpster fire." Community leaders are pushing for aggressive pike suppression efforts to restore the ecosystem.

5
Latest ESA Rule Change Threatens Spotted Owls, Panthers, and Gray Wolves

The rescission of the "harm" definition puts species like Mexican Spotted Owls, Florida panthers, and California condors at heightened risk of extinction. Under the new rule, federal agencies can no longer block habitat-destructive projects like logging on owl nesting grounds or mining in panther corridors unless the action directly kills individual animals. Conservation biologists note the prior 50-year interpretation was upheld by the U.S. Supreme Court in 1986 and has successfully prevented extinction for 99% of listed species. The Trump administration justified the change by citing the recent Loper Bright v. Raimondo Supreme Court decision.

6
Deforestation in Belize Triggers Flash Floods as Villages Lose Tree Protection

Heavy rains in Belize's deforested regions have caused severe flooding in surrounding villages, demonstrating how habitat loss amplifies climate impacts. Engineers have documented that trees slow water flow, stabilize soil, and channel rainwater into aquifers. Without forest cover, even moderate rainfall becomes destructive, overwhelming drainage systems and causing mudslides. The incident underscores how deforestation exacerbates both biodiversity loss and human vulnerability to extreme weather, with vulnerable communities bearing the brunt of ecosystem destruction profits from timber and agricultural expansion.

7
Four in Five Americans Support Core Endangered Species Act Mission

Recent polling commissioned by conservation groups shows 80% of Americans support the Endangered Species Act's core protections, even as the Trump administration moves to weaken enforcement. Environmental organizations are leveraging the strong public support in legal challenges to the new rule and in appeals to Congress. Support crosses party lines and demonstrates that Americans prioritize wildlife and habitat protection alongside economic interests. Environmental advocates expect legal challenges to the revised rule to reach federal courts within weeks.

Today's Briefing
1
Over 300 Global Scientists Unite on Wildlife and Climate

At the 64th Sessions of the UNFCCC Subsidiary Bodies in Bonn, 287 leading scientists from six continents endorsed the Scientific Consensus on Wildlife and Climate, calling on governments to explicitly incorporate wild animals into climate policies. Research shows that restoring wild animal populations and their ecological roles could increase CO₂ uptake by an additional 6.41 gigatons per year—a critical step toward meeting climate targets. From elephants dispersing seeds to marine species storing carbon in the ocean, animals are essential allies in ecosystem resilience.

2
EU Rule Meant to Curb Illegal Logging Actually Increased Deforestation

A new study by researchers at the Wyss Academy for Nature reveals that the EU Timber Regulation (EUTR), which took effect in 2013, led to unintended consequences: forest loss in trade-dependent countries rose 52.4% compared to what would have occurred without the rule—an estimated 272,640 square kilometers of additional forest loss between 2010 and 2019. Importers wary of compliance risks abandoned entire countries with weak governance, redirecting timber to less regulated markets like China or pushing producers to clear forest for agriculture instead.

3
Endangered Species Act Celebrates 50 Years With 99% Survival Rate

The U.S. Endangered Species Act, signed in 1973, has protected more than 2,400 plants and animals, with an extraordinary 99% of those species still in existence today. Since 1973, 55 species have recovered enough to be delisted—including the bald eagle and giant panda—while another 56 have been downlisted from endangered to threatened status. Despite its remarkable track record, the law has grown politically controversial on Capitol Hill and faces ongoing challenges from shifting political winds.

4
Deadly H5N1 Bird Flu Confirmed in Australia for First Time

H5N1 avian influenza has been confirmed in Australia, marking the virus's arrival on the continent for the first time. Scientists documented the pathogen in an infected petrel and confirmed a second case in South Australia, raising concerns about transmission to wildlife and domestic animals. While the H5N1 strain can be fatal to cats and dogs, human cases remain rare and the disease can often be mistaken for other illnesses in pets, experts say.

5
Colossal Biosciences & US Fish and Wildlife Launch Historic Endangered Species Biobank

Colossal Biosciences and the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service announced on June 25 a landmark partnership to collect, sequence, and cryopreserve genetic material from all 2,100+ species on the U.S. Endangered Species List. The open-source biobank will preserve a complete genomic record that can be used either to engineer greater resilience into living populations or support future de-extinction efforts. The effort responds to stark reality: only 54 species have recovered enough to be delisted in the past 50 years, while 67 have gone extinct despite protection.

6
Marine Expedition Uncovers 31 New Species in Two Weeks Off Brazil

A marine research expedition along the coast of Brazil discovered 31 previously unknown species in just two weeks, including a transparent glass squid with distinctive yellow-orange spots. The rapid pace of discovery highlights both the biodiversity of the ocean and the urgency of marine conservation—many ecosystems remain barely explored, and species are disappearing before science can even document them.

7
Scientist Wins $100K Prize for Decoding Birdsong

A researcher has won a $100,000 award for groundbreaking work on understanding and decoding bird communication. The achievement represents a major step toward bridging the gap between humans and wildlife, opening possibilities for better monitoring of avian populations and understanding how birds respond to environmental change and habitat loss. The prize highlights growing investment in technology that helps scientists understand—and ultimately protect—the natural world.

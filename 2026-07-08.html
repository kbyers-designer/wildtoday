Indonesia Develops Roadmap to Recognize Indigenous Knowledge in Biodiversity Conservation

The Indonesian government is drafting a roadmap to protect and promote Indigenous peoples' and local communities' (IPLCs) traditional knowledge in biodiversity conservation, aligning with commitments to the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework. Indigenous territories already safeguard an estimated 29 million hectares (71.6 million acres) of biodiverse ecosystems through customary governance systems such as sasi in Maluku and awig-awig in Bali. However, only 1 million hectares have been formally documented, leaving many communities vulnerable to having their conservation efforts overlooked or criminalized despite generations of land stewardship.

Critical Desert Springs Biodiversity Hotspots Face Extinction Crisis, UC San Diego Study Warns

A comprehensive UC San Diego study of over 1,100 springs in the American West reveals that deep-sourced regional aquifer springs support more than 150 endemic species of fish, mollusks, crustaceans, and aquatic insects found nowhere else on Earth. Unfortunately, over 65% of springs in the Great Basin and Mojave Deserts have been severely degraded, and at least 16 endemic species have gone extinct since the 1880s. The research identifies groundwater pumping, mining, and urban development as primary threats to these fragile oases of biodiversity.

North Carolina Appropriates Historic $10.2 Million for Wildlife Crossings

North Carolina's General Assembly has appropriated $10.2 million in recurring funding for wildlife crossings and mitigation strategies, marking a historic commitment to reducing vehicle-wildlife collisions and habitat fragmentation. The investment builds on a 2023 landmark appropriation and establishes a permanent funding source to implement underpasses, overpasses, and associated fencing for species like black bears and elk in the biologically diverse state. Wildlife crossings have been proven to dramatically reduce collisions while maintaining critical habitat connectivity for long-term species survival.

4
Colorado Furbearer Management Lacks Science-Based Limits, Project Coyote Report Finds

Project Coyote's analysis of Colorado Parks & Wildlife data reveals that the state's "unlimited killing" policy for furbearers lacks scientific justification. During the 2023-24 season, just five hunters killed 142 of 172 pine martens, and two people accounted for 43 of 92 swift foxes, with no limits on kills, no possession limits, and no requirement to report kill locations. The report calls for kill limits, a closed coyote season, mandatory location reporting, and species-specific population assessments to align Colorado's wildlife management with conservation science and public values.

5
Seychelles Plants 1,207 Coral Fragments in Major Reef Restoration Initiative

The Seychelles Parks and Gardens Authority (SPGA) and Absa Seychelles completed a significant coral restoration project in Ste. Anne Marine Park, carefully outplanting 1,207 coral fragments onto structures made from confiscated recycled fish traps. The initiative, which reflects the power of public-private partnerships, supports both climate mitigation and adaptation as healthy coral reefs act as natural barriers, support fisheries and tourism, and build ecosystem resilience against climate change impacts. This marks the third collaborative restoration effort under a partnership formalized in 2024.

6
Federal Soil Conservation Agency Faces Steep Job Cuts, Threatening Farmer Support Services

The Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS), which has supported farmers since the Dust Bowl era of the 1930s, lost more than 2,500 of its 12,000 employees in the first six months of last year, and further cuts have been proposed. Farmers in Louisiana and across the country report difficulty accessing technical assistance for conservation practices like cover crops, soil health improvements, and water protection. NRCS employs scientists and engineers who visit farms to ensure conservation practices are properly implemented, and the loss of institutional knowledge threatens the agency's ability to serve agricultural communities effectively.

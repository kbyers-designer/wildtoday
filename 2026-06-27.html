Today's Briefing
1
Marine expedition uncovers 31 new species off Brazil

Scientists working in the ocean midwater off Brazil used cutting-edge technology to discover 31 new species in just two weeks, including transparent glass squid and other remarkable lifeforms. The expedition achieved near-record discovery speeds, with a team of marine experts working at depths where barely any exploration has occurred. The discoveries highlight the incredible biodiversity of the deep ocean and the importance of continued research in Earth's least-explored ecosystems.

2
Federal government partners to preserve genetic material from 2,300 endangered species

The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service announced a partnership with Colossal Biosciences to create a BioVault — a cryogenic repository of genetic material, tissue samples, and DNA from roughly 2,300 endangered and threatened plants and wildlife. The effort, announced June 25, aims to preserve genetic diversity before species lose critical genetic information or disappear entirely, potentially enabling future assisted reproduction and recovery programs. The samples will be stored at -321 degrees Fahrenheit at locations including Colossal's Dallas headquarters.

3
Ecuador's mangrove forests destroyed to expand shrimp farming

The world's voracious appetite for shrimp is driving massive destruction of Ecuador's mangrove ecosystems, vital habitats that support countless species and protect coastal communities. Industrial shrimp farming operations are clearing mangrove forests at an alarming rate, threatening the ecological stability of the region and the livelihoods of traditional communities. The Guardian's reporting reveals how global seafood demand directly fuels habitat loss in one of South America's most biodiverse regions.

4
Honduras mobilizes armed forces to combat deforestation by 2029

Honduras launched its Zero Deforestation by 2029 plan in May 2024, declaring a state of emergency for its forests and mobilizing an environmental protection battalion of 8,000 troops. The initiative focuses on retaking control of protected areas where criminal groups have engaged in illegal timber trafficking, mining, and agriculture. However, experts caution that militarization alone is insufficient without broader investments in public policy and legal enforcement to address the root causes of forest loss.

5
World's rarest marine mammal preserved through digital imaging

Scientists have successfully preserved the skeleton of a vaquita porpoise, the world's rarest marine mammal with fewer than 10 individuals remaining, using advanced digital imaging technology. The vaquita, native to Mexico's Gulf of California, faces extinction from accidental entanglement in fishing nets and illegal gillnets used for another endangered species, the totoaba. The digital preservation effort represents a novel approach to safeguarding critical biological data even as species approach extinction.

6
Scientist rediscovers bat species feared extinct in Nigerian sanctuary

Biologist Iroro Tanshi rediscovered the short-tailed roundleaf bat in Nigeria's Afi sanctuary, a species that was feared extinct. Weighing about as much as a teaspoon of salt, this tiny bat represents one of the world's most vulnerable species, with only one known roosting colony. Tanshi's work highlights both the potential for conservation success through dedicated fieldwork and the fragility of species with extremely limited distributions.

7
Scientist wins $100,000 prize for breakthrough in decoding birdsong

A researcher has won a prestigious $100,000 prize for developing methods to decode and understand birdsong communication, bringing humans closer to understanding how birds communicate with one another. The breakthrough uses advanced analysis techniques to identify patterns and meanings in bird vocalizations, opening new avenues for wildlife research and monitoring. The discovery has applications for bird conservation efforts and deepens our understanding of animal cognition.

8
Data center project near Nashville Zoo threatens endangered species habitat

A proposed data center near Nashville Zoo is facing federal scrutiny over threats to the habitat of the Nashville crayfish, a species found nowhere else on Earth and listed under the Endangered Species Act. Environmental groups warn that the project, located just over a quarter-mile from occupied crayfish habitat, could harm water quality and the ecosystem through stormwater runoff and thermal impacts. The facility also threatens habitat for other rare species including the proposed endangered eastern hellbender and other vulnerable animals.
